# The Rise of the Social

> The rise of the social is Arendt's name for the modern expansion of household-like necessity into public life. It matters here because it gives a non-libertarian way to criticize administrative politics without reducing the critique to markets versus government.

## Human Condition

In [[the-human-condition|The Human Condition]], Arendt argues that ancient distinctions between household necessity and public freedom become blurred in modernity. The social realm treats collective life as a large-scale household to be managed through behavior, welfare, economy, and administration.

## Revolution

In [[on-revolution|On Revolution]], the same concern appears as the social question. Arendt thinks revolutions can lose political freedom when necessity overwhelms founding and participation. That judgment is controversial, but it is coherent enough for a concept page because it cuts across the new Arendt sources.

## Use in This Wiki

The concept helps separate libertarian and Arendtian critiques. Libertarians often criticize state welfare and planning because they violate property, prices, and voluntary exchange. Arendt worries that administration of life-process displaces the public realm where freedom appears.
