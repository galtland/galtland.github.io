# Council System

> Council system names Arendt's recurring revolutionary form of local, participatory self-government. In [[on-revolution|On Revolution]] it is a lost treasure: repeatedly rediscovered in revolutions, repeatedly displaced by parties, bureaucracy, or centralized state forms.

## Concept

[[on-revolution|On Revolution]] treats councils as institutions through which ordinary people attempt to preserve public freedom after liberation. They are not merely advisory committees; they are spaces of action, deliberation, and federated political participation.

Arendt's claim is that this form keeps reappearing — spontaneously and without coordination — whenever a revolution opens space for public action, only to be displaced each time by professional parties and centralized administration. Her recurring instances are concrete: the revolutionary societies and sections of the French Revolution, the Paris Commune of 1871, the Russian soviets of 1905 and 1917, the German workers' and soldiers' councils (Räte) of 1918–1919, and the councils of the 1956 Hungarian Revolution (neighborhood, revolutionary, worker, and student councils alike) — and, as the constitutional road not taken, Jefferson's ward system, his "elementary republics" meant to give every citizen a tangible place to act. Her sixth chapter names this the revolutionary tradition's "lost treasure": a genuine experience of public freedom the tradition repeatedly produced but never managed to institutionalize. Her tone is sympathetic but stops short of a worked-out institutional blueprint.

## Libertarian Caution

The council system should not be forced into market anarchism or private-law theory. It is a republican and participatory concept. Its relevance is that it gives the wiki a non-state-centered way to think about public action without making market exchange carry every meaning of freedom.
