# For the New Intellectual

> [[for-the-new-intellectual|For the New Intellectual]] is [[ayn-rand|Ayn Rand]]'s 1961 first nonfiction *book* (after shorter earlier pieces such as the 1946 pamphlet *Textbook of Americanism*). Its long title essay argues that Western culture has been ruled by an alliance of two anti-reason archetypes — "Attila," the man of force, and "the Witch Doctor," the man of faith and mysticism — and calls for a new kind of intellectual who champions reason; the rest of the book gathers the major philosophical passages from her four novels.

## The Title Essay

Rand's historical thesis is that the man of production and reason has, across most of history, been dominated by two parasitic types who divide the spoils between them. "Attila" rules by physical force — the warlord, the dictator, the state that seizes. "The Witch Doctor" rules by claims on the supernatural and the moral — the mystic who tells the producer that his earthly life and reason are corrupt and that he owes obedience and sacrifice. The two are presented as complementary: force needs a moral sanction, and mysticism needs an enforcer.

Against this pairing Rand sets the producer — the man whose tool is reason — and argues that civilization advances only when the producer is free and morally vindicated. The "new intellectual" she calls for is one who breaks the historic alliance by defending reason as an absolute, refusing both the rule of force and the rule of unreason, and grounding culture in [[objectivism|Objectivism]]'s commitments to reality, reason, and rational self-interest.

## The Anthology

More than half of the book is an anthology: Rand reprints the principal philosophical speeches and passages from her fiction so that the abstract argument of the title essay can be read alongside its dramatized form. The selections are drawn from all four novels — [[we-the-living|We the Living]], [[anthem|Anthem]], [[the-fountainhead|The Fountainhead]], and [[atlas-shrugged|Atlas Shrugged]] — and include the best-known set-pieces such as Roark's courtroom defense of the creator, Francisco's speech on money, and the philosophical core of Galt's radio address.

## Place in Rand's Work

*For the New Intellectual* is the hinge between Rand the novelist and Rand the systematic philosopher. It is her first book-length statement of her position directly to the reader rather than through characters, and it set up the run of non-fiction collections — [[the-virtue-of-selfishness|The Virtue of Selfishness]] (1964) on ethics and [[capitalism-the-unknown-ideal|Capitalism: The Unknown Ideal]] (1966) on politics — that followed. Because much of the volume is excerpted fiction, it also works as a compact entry point to the philosophy dramatized across the novels.
