# We the Living

> [[we-the-living|We the Living]] is [[ayn-rand|Ayn Rand]]'s 1936 first novel, set in Petrograd in the early years of Soviet rule. Through the fate of the engineering student Kira Argounova and the two men around her, it dramatizes Rand's earliest and most personal theme: the destruction of the individual by a state that claims the right to dispose of every life for the collective.

## The Story

Kira Argounova returns with her dispossessed bourgeois family to a Petrograd remade by the Bolshevik revolution. She wants to build — to become an engineer — in a society that has abolished the space for private ambition. She loves Leo Kovalensky, the son of a persecuted aristocrat, and is loved in turn by Andrei Taganov, an idealistic Communist and GPU officer. The triangle is the vehicle for the book's argument rather than its point: each character is a way of testing what the collectivist state does to people who try to live by their own values. The novel ends in tragedy, with Kira shot attempting to cross the border to freedom.

## The Argument

Rand later stressed that the novel is not primarily a story about Soviet Russia. Russia supplies the setting and the firsthand detail — Rand had lived through the revolution and emigrated in 1926 — but the thesis is general: the supremacy of the individual over the collective, and the claim that a system organized around the sacrifice of the individual to the state cannot help but consume its best people. Andrei, the sincere Communist, is as much a victim of the system's premises as Kira and Leo; his disillusionment is one of the book's sharpest devices.

Compared with the later novels, *We the Living* is the least schematic and the most emotionally direct. The fully worked-out philosophy of [[objectivism|Objectivism]] is not yet present, but its moral center — the individual life as an end in itself, not a means to the collective — already organizes the book.

## Place in Rand's Work and This Wiki

*We the Living* is the first statement of the anti-collectivism that runs through [[anthem|Anthem]], [[the-fountainhead|The Fountainhead]], and [[atlas-shrugged|Atlas Shrugged]]. For this wiki it sits alongside the non-libertarian witnesses to Soviet collectivism: where [[the-gulag-archipelago|Solzhenitsyn]] documents the camp system and [[totalitarianism|Arendt]] theorizes the regime form, Rand renders the everyday extinction of private life under the same system in fiction.

A first revised edition appeared in 1959. Rand made stylistic cuts and trimmed a few passages whose phrasing she felt could be misread as Nietzschean; the 1959 text is the one most commonly read today.
